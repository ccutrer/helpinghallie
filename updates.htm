    • October 19, 2011

    Hallie is out of surgery and doing great. Things went better than we could have hoped for. She was back there longer than expected, but they told us it took them almost an hour to prep her and get her in the right position, so I guess 3 hours total isn't too bad. She woke up pretty groggy and out of it, although one of the first things she did was sign "I love you" to me in recovery, followed quickly after by her moving her pointer finger up and down. It took a little while to figure out what she meant, but she was wanting to play with Grammy's iPad. She is in a constant care room for the next day, but is doing phenomenally. We have had so many of her doctors and nurses comment on how well she is doing. Of course, they loaded her with pain meds during surgery, but most of them are wearing off and she is doing just fine. We are alternating tylenol and the iv form of ibuprofen just to be safe, but I am surprised at how little pain she is in. I guess when you are in so much pain every day, a little extra isn't a big deal. She did tell me that her legs don't hurt for the first time in almost a year, so we are very hopeful that this will alleviate at least the pain in her lower extremities. Hallie is eating, drinking, playing, and we have even heard a few laughs out of her and seen more than a few smiles. All in all, we are very pleased with how things have gone so far.

    Thank you all for your thoughts and prayers! I know they have played a huge role in how well things have gone and I am extremely grateful for them.

    • October 15, 2011

    Hallie's surgery is on for Wednesday. We had to meet with the GI doctor, pediatrician, and neurologist this week and I am very glad that is over with! Hallie's labs look so much better this week, which is a big relief. The last thing Hallie needs is to go into surgery with her chemistry levels all messed up. Her pediatrician is concerned with how much pain Hallie is on all over her body. She is wondering if Hallie has developed a secondary condition as a result of her chronic pain, like maybe some form of myalgia. Our visit to the pediatrician was on the day that Hallie screamed for 5 hours. That was a very long day! Nothing seemed to make her happy until she finally fell asleep and when she woke up, she still was pretty emotional, so I gave her some pain meds and she finally started feeling better. The pediatrician wants to wait until after this surgery for her tethered cord and see if she is still in as much pain. If she is, we are going to start testing her for a few of these possible secondary conditions.

    We met with the new neurologist yesterday. I had pinned a lot of hopes on him being able to figure Hallie's headaches out. Of all the pain that she is in, her headaches are by far the worst and no one has been able to give me any idea of why she may be having them. I was more than a little disappointed and discouraged after meeting with the neurologist. I also cried during the visit, something that I rarely do. He is convinced Hallie's headaches are caused by stress. I don't doubt that they contribute to them as I get some pretty bad tension headaches. I just have a hard time believing that none of her physical problems play a part in the headaches, especially her malformations in her cervical vertebrae. He wants me to reduce my stress (umm, sure-- you do realize I have a daughter with major medical problems, right?) and reduce Hallie's stress. He wants us to see a new psychologist to help Hallie cope better. I am all for getting her help. I took her to counseling for 6 months and felt like it was a big waste of time and money. The counselor we saw let Hallie play for the whole time we were in there. I understand they do a lot of play therapy at this age, but there was no direction given, no asking her questions. Hallie just played and drew random pictures for 6 months. So I am more than willing to look into a different therapist. However, the neurologist wants me to drive down town to see one. That means me finding someone to watch my other kids, trying to find a time to do it that doesn't take Hallie out of school, which typically means me having to travel during rush hour, which means about 2 hours there and back, sometimes more. To me, it sounds like even more stress. I'm willing to do it for a few times, but it just isn't realistic to do long term. In the end, he cleared her for surgery, which is what we needed, but I'm not sure how much confidence I have in this neurologist. I would much prefer him to say that he didn't know exactly what was causing the headaches, but it was most likely a combination of a few different things going on in her body, exacerbated by stress, not just blaming it completely on hers and my stress. The fact that he felt that she might have a syndrome that is characterized by craniofacial anomalies, didn't instill a whole lot of confidence in him. I wanted to ask him if he had actually looked at my daughter.

    At this point, we are going ahead with surgery on Wednesday. She will be in the hospital until at least Sunday. I feel like she has so many things wrong that how can you begin to figure out what is causing which pain. The only way is to start removing the things we know are wrong from the equation to see what is left behind. And if nothing else, it will relieve some stress in both of our lives since we have known this surgery needed to happen since April. We will be very glad to have it behind us! We will see what she is like after and decide where we go from here. We will definitely be looking for a new counselor to help her cope better and may even start looking into a pain specialist. In November, we will be meeting with a geneticist and hope that he can give us a better idea of what is wrong with Hallie.

    Just a quick reminder, tomorrow is the day that we are asking people to join us in fasting and/or prayer on Hallie's behalf. We appreciate all of your love and support as we deal with all of the challenges ahead of us.

We also added a dietary supplement that is just carbs and fats to try and help in the weight gain department. She has gained a few ounces in the last week, so we will take it. Regardless of whether it is surgery or more tests to figure out what is going on, I am tired of waiting around while she suffers. Let's just get going already!

    • August 29, 2011

    It's been a few weeks of many ups and downs. Hallie went in 2 1/2 weeks ago and had a cerebral arteriogram done. They put her out, made an incision in her leg and went up through an artery into the 4 different vertebral arteries and injected dye into each. They did an MRI with her neck in both extension and flexion. It took about 2 1/2 hours and when the doctor came out, he said that everything looked really good except for one of her vertebral arteries starts shunting blood away from her brain when extended backwards. He didn't think it was a major issue, so he just wants to follow up in a year for more imaging. The recovery was a lot more in depth than I anticipated. They hadn't told me that she would have to lie completely flat for 6 hours after surgery to prevent the artery in her leg from bleeding. She threw up a lot after coming out of the anesthesia and the only reason they discharged her was that the couldn't get her iv reflux meds in the surgical observation unit and we desperately needed them! Hallie slept with me and threw up a good portion of the night. She woke up throwing up and didn't stop until about 7:30 the next morning. She wasn't allowed to do any physical activity and she couldn't even walk up or down the stairs. She was not a happy camper! She felt pretty rugged, but she is so used to pushing through it all that it was so hard for her to have me keep telling her friends that Hallie couldn't play. She finally convinced me to pull her wheelchair out and take her for a walk around the block so that she wouldn't miss anything. We sat outside for awhile so that she could visit with her friends. The cute neighbor girls across the street that are Carson's age came over and made friendship bracelets with her. She has quite the collection of them now! I have been very frustrated since her tests. You are told your child might be a ticking time bomb, so you get anxious to act and do whatever needs to be done, but my definition of timely manner and the medical field's is completely different. Hallie has 2 neurosurgeons and it seems that every time I talk to the nurse and ask a question, she has to go to one neurosurgeon and then that one has to go to the other and confer, at which point they go back to the nurse, who, in turn, calls me. Then, when my question isn't answered completely, we have had to start the process all over. I feel like I am juggling a three ring circus! It has been very frustrating, especially since Hallie was getting ready to start school and they wanted to know what was going on and I couldn't tell them. Finally, the neurosurgeons set up a time when they could both be there, so Derrick and I headed downtown to meet with them. Dr. Alden (the structural specialist) told us that the artery that is acting like a shunt is draining into her epidural space and then the blood is being absorbed. He said that is the most ideal place to drain, if it is going to happen, since it won't cause any damage. He said there are a number of other places it could drain that would all be problematic. He pointed out more anomalies in her C1 vertebrae. In a normal C1, there are two spinal processes that stick up to provide stability. Hallie's right one is normal, but her left one is almost lateral. She has very little range of motion (not past about 30 degrees) when turning her head to the left. Dr. Alden asked if she had torticallis when she was a baby and I told him, yes, we did extensive physical therapy for it. He is thinking that had less to do with the muscles in her neck and more to do with her abnormalities in her vertebrae. It's eye opening to find yet another abnormality, but at the same time, it is nice to have explanations for something she struggles with. Answers are always so much nicer to find than more questions! From his perspective, we will do some imaging in 6 months on her neck and then an MRI in a year. His biggest concern is that the discs in her neck will have a lot more pressure and wear and tear than normal. He says you don't see many degenerative discs until people are in the their 50's-60's, but he anticipates Hallie's will start to degenerate a lot quicker, so it will be very important to follow up regularly. Dr. Bowman (the nerve specialist) is still very concerned by Hallie's migraines. She is getting one almost every single day. Some last a few hours, some can last all day. She wants us to go see the Chief Neurologist before putting Hallie back on the schedule for surgery. As a neurosurgeon, they focus more on mechanics and she wants someone that will focus more on the function and see if we can't figure out what is causing these. This neurologist deals with highly complex cases on a regular basis, so we are hoping that he will be able to help us find some answers. I am waiting for a phone call back from his office. They have already gotten the ok to put her in ASAP, but we are just waiting to see when that will be. After we meet with him, Dr. Bowman will decide if we can get Hallie's surgery back on the schedule again. This all moves so maddeningly slow, especially since we were supposed to have this fixed by now. It is so hard to have her wake up and tell me her legs, back, tummy, and head hurt and wonder how much of that pain is from her tethered cord that shouldn't be a problem anymore. She had a rough weekend and that doesn't help matters. But, the more logical part of me is so grateful to have doctors that listen and are taking every precaution and truly looking for the cause so that we can really help Hallie. As a result of all of her throwing up after being under anesthesia, Hallie has been coughing a lot more. The last few days it changed from the tight wheezing to a really junky cough. I took her in the pediatrician after the hospital and she was diagnosed with a sinus infection and upper respiratory infection. We are very lucky it didn't turn into pneumonia. They think she may have aspirated a little while vomiting. So, she is on antibiotics and nebulizers to try and clear her up. I am praying that she responds well, without any secondary problems since we are really working on getting her back onto the surgery schedule quickly. On a more positive note, Hallie started Kindergarten on Wednesday. I can't tell you how many times I have had to not let myself think of kindergarten over the years because I wasn't sure if it would ever be a possibility. The school has been amazing at making sure Hallie's needs are being met. She has her own supplies and the janitor comes and cleans everything, including the bathroom before Hallie's class starts. She has special snack times with the principal and they have been great to provide acitivities that she can do outside when the kids are on the playground. They are working on hiring an instructional assistant to oversee her care at school so that the teacher can focus more on the other kids. Her teacher has been great, even stopping by one evening to touch base on a few different things and give us her home phone number. I couldn't ask for better! And Hallie is loving it. She is meeting new friends and has many of her neighborhood friends in class as well. She loves to learn and is so excited to go each day and so what they will be doing each day. Thank you all for your continued support through all of this!

  • Surgery Update
    • August 10, 2011

    Today was Hallie's surgery. Or at least, it was supposed to be her surgery to repair her tethered spinal cord. We left the house at 7:30 this morning and finally made it back home at 5:00 pm. And we didn't do a single thing the whole day, other than wait in an exam room. The first surgery on the schedule took our surgeon much longer to complete than scheduled for (around 4 hours longer). So we sat, and we sat, and we sat. The surgeon finally came in and asked how Hallie had been feeling. I told her that the migraines are getting worse and more frequent. Yesterday, Hallie had the worst migraine she has ever had. She threw up her pain meds twice. She screamed and cried most of the morning. I tried heat, cold, even those Ben-Gay pain patches. She threw up 6-7 times. And of course, with her reflux, her throat was burning and she started coughing and wheezing. She slept for a little while fitfully and then woke up and threw up some more. She cried and told me she didn't want to be special anymore. It isn't that much fun. I cried most of the morning because there was not one thing I could do to help her. I almost took her to the hospital so they could give her some iv pain meds. It was a nightmare! She had one of these headaches a week or so ago, although not quite as bad, but up to that point, the worst she had experienced.

    So, after talking to the neurosurgeon about this, she was concerned. I don't blame her. I have been concerned. I have talked to nurses and doctors about this, but evidently they were not passing the information onto Dr. Bowman. She went and studied Hallie's CT and MRI images some more, made a few phone calls and came back to ask more questions. I discussed with her that my home health nurse and I think that a lot of Hallie's migraines seem to be caused by the position of her head. If she tilts it forward just a little bit, you can watch the color drain out of her face and the migraine immediately hits. Hallie has been getting migraines just from riding in the car. The head rest seems to push her head forward just enough to cause a lot of pain. I carry ziploc bags in the car for her to throw up in since she seems to if she is in the car for longer than 20-30 minutes. This concerned Dr. Bowman even more so she went back and made a few more phone calls. Then she came and called Derrick and I out of the room to discuss our options.

    The other neurosurgeon that specializes more in the bone structure is concerned that when Hallie's head is tipped forward just a bit, it is pinching a vertebral artery. This is a huge concern is the fact that her surgery has to be done with her being on her stomach for 2 hours. She never sleeps on her stomach. She can't move her head from side to side very well. Dr. Bowman is thinking these are natural reflexes to protect herself. If they put her to sleep, they take away her natural reflexes. When they turned her to her stomach, she could have a stroke before they could do a thing about it. We all felt the risk was just too high. Especially, since this theory makes so much sense to me. It is the first time that they have given me a possible explanation that explains all of the symptoms and severity of them. A big concern is that they are coming a lot more frequently with increasing intensity. So obviously what is causing them is getting worse.

    Where do we go from here? We are still working on all of that, but Dr. Bowman has called what is called an interventional radiology specialist. He works at all of the hospitals in Chicagoland, both adult and pediatric. He specializes in finding ways to prove doctors theories with radiologic proof. From the conversation Dr. Bowman had with him, he is thinking of doing imaging with an angiogram to show the arteries and sensors on her neck to detect the potential problems as they have her move her neck back and forth. They are also hoping to do a MRI while she is awake (good luck!) so they can get her to moved her head during the imaging. The two neurosurgeons and the interventionalist will be conferring tomorrow to decide which tests will give them the needed information. There is the chance that it will show nothing, in which case we will do the tethered cord release as soon as possible. If it does show something, we will most likely be doing emergent surgery to repair her vertebrae. This is an ugly surgery and has a lot of potential risks. We are hoping to avoid it, but it seems that all of her symptoms are pointing towards it needing to be repaired. Hopefully, we will have a better idea of when we can get the tests run by tomorrow. Dr. Bowman is going to try and get it pushed through as an emergency, so we shall see.

    We are all tired and frustrated. It has been a long road to get to this point and we were so ready to be done with this surgery. The anxiety and nerves building up to this were for naught and we now will get to go through it all again. My mom flew in from Utah to help me entertain Hallie during her hospitalization. Hallie's first question after they postponed the surgery was if Grammy was still going to spend the night. Now, she is busy making plans of what to do with Grammy since we aren't in the hospital. She is itching to dig into the bag of things to entertain her, although I have told her she needs to save them for the hospital. My in-laws came up from Kentucky and were planning on taking my boys back with them today. They ended up taking Tucker with them, since we hope the next week or so will involve tests and possible surgery. He was totally thrilled to be going with them. He was grinning and waving as they drove off with him in tow. Carson decided to stay at home. He is starting school in 2 weeks and wanted to stay and play with friends. He is a little easier to deal with in a hospital setting! We are going with the flow and hoping for the best!

    Thank you for all of your kind words and most importantly prayers. While we are frustrated with the delay, we are hoping and praying that the doctor is being guided to do what is best for Hallie. We felt the risks were far too high without further imaging. We have been overwhelmed by the support and concern on our behalf. Thank you all so much!

    • July 19, 2011

    Hallie was in the hospital last week for her endoscopy and pH probe study. The doctors decided at last minute not to do the bronchoscopy since they are very confident that her asthma is caused by her reflux. The endoscopy showed that the bottom portion of her esophagus is fried. It is so red, the pictures made me cry. The pH probe is a tube that went in her nose and down into her esophagus. It was connected to a little recorder that read the pH levels. Anything below 4 is considered acidic. Hallie dropped below that a few times during the day, but the minute she fell asleep at night, it dropped down from between 2.6-1.3. That is somewhere between hydrochloric acid and battery acid. It was a little scary to watch how low it was dropping. We had to keep a diary of her activity and reflux symptoms throughout the day for the doctor to match up with the computer readings and interpret it all.

    I just got Hallie's pH probe results back. She has SEVERE reflux (not that I didn't already know that, but it's nice for the doctors to have it confirmed!) The doctor and I both agree that Hallie needs to have a surgery on her stomach to prevent her from refluxing. The doctor is baffled by why Hallie's reflux keeps getting worse as most kids have outgrown their reflux by this age. But unfortunately, iv meds are supposed to be used as a short term solution to get the reflux under control and Hallie has been on them for over a year. I have a call in to the surgeon's office to schedule a consult so that we can discuss the risks. Of course, with Hallie it is much more complicated than usual, with her scar tissue and organs being misplaced, so before we go ahead with the Nissen surgery, the GI doctor wants us to be well aware of the risks.

    On the positive side of this, Hallie seems to be holding her own so far without TPN and once she has this surgery done, we won't have need for a port. They will leave it in for several months afterwards to make sure that she won't need it, but it won't have to be accessed. I have been accessing my daughter with a needle every Sunday night for a year and a half. It will be so nice to not have to do that anymore! It also means that Hallie will be able to bathe every day and swim whenever she wants. I guess I should look into swim lessons for her-- it has not been a very realistic thing to try before. She is going to be so excited to feel a little bit more normal. And the hope is that by eliminating her refluxing, she won't need to be on 3 different meds to control her asthma. As much as I hate to submit her to more surgery, this one has the potential of making our lives a LOT less complicated! I'm excited to see what her life is like after these next few surgeries. We still have to potential of one more surgery-- the one for her fused vertebrae, but right now, we are trying to eliminate the things that we know need to be fixed so that we can see how much of her pain is due to her vertebrae. With her having so many different problems, causing all sorts of pain, it is hard to determine what is causing which pain. So by eliminating what we do know is contributing to some o her pain, we should have a better picture of how much pain her vertebrae are causing her. We will make a decision about that surgery after we get through the next two.

    I have been in contact with a group for children with undiagnosed syndromes.. A lot of them feel that Hallie has some sort of syndrome that would explain all of her birth defects. How many serious birth defects can one child have? At least ones that are seemingly unconnected. We will be going to a geneticist in November and start the genetic testing. I have had several people ask me why it matters that we go through all of these tests. So I will tell you why we feel it is so important. First, we need to know if there are other problems that we should be looking for. Are there things that are causing long term damage that may not have presented themselves yet? We hope not, and with the number of tests that she has undergone and her weekly lab draws, we think it is more of a long shot, but we just have to know for sure. I don't want to be constantly wondering when something new is going to pop up. If we can get her to a point that Hallie is more stable, I want to be able to enjoy every second of it, without fear of something else going wrong. Our other big motivation is that if we can get an overall diagnosis, maybe we can get better support. We have been in contact with many families who have kids facing different degrees of health problems. Some are annoying, but can be managed easily, and others are life threatening or terminal. I envy those that have found huge support with families that are dealing with the same diagnosis. In many instances, they have become great friends and in some cases, like family as they are able to truly empathize and relate to the challenges in their life. We don't know a single person that has Hallie's same symptoms. We don't have a foundation that helps us navigate Hallie's battles. We are struggling to get the school district to put a nurse in her classroom, despite the teacher and principal agreeing that it would be best. I spent an hour on the phone today with the state Department of Specialized Children's Needs. While a small portion of Hallie's needs fall in their guidelines, they are concerned about her all needs are being met and think that an overall diagnosis would help facilitate that.

    I took the kids to see the dentist last week. I am struggling to find the balance between all of Hallie's needs and the rest of our normal needs. Hallie couldn't get her teeth cleaned (we didn't have her on antibiotics prior to coming in for her heart problems) but they did x-rays and examined her teeth. She has 4 cavities (her teeth have a lot of problems courtesy of her reflux). She had 2 that I knew needed to be filled but haven't been able to get it scheduled in between everything else and 1 that they had been watching. We switched to a new dentist because of some problems we ran into with the last one, so we had to go over all of her medical problems. The dentist wanted to put Hallie under general anesthesia to fill the cavities. Really? She obviously doesn't understand how complicated putting her out is. We have to have clearance from the cardiologist, pulmonologist, and GI. She has to go on steroids and antibiotics. Not to mention that recovery is HORRIBLE. The GI doctor mentioned it to me today. She told me that she knew I had told her she gets really upset after anesthesia, but she didn't realize how bad it gets until she saw it herself. We will most likely keep Hallie sedated after her tethered cord surgery so that she doesn't do any damage to herself. So, I told the dentist that general anesthesia was not the best option. We are going to attempt to fill them without. They are going to give her laughing gas, which ought to be very interesting to see how she does with. Hopefully, it will go smoothly. The scheduler looked at me like I was from another planet when she was trying to find a date. Hallie has so much scheduled already and then Carson has just been diagnosed with ADHD, so we have lots of appointments for him, and Tucker I think has hearing loss, so he is going in for a hearing test and to see the ENT. He is also due to have his eyes checked, since he has a droopy eyelid that the weight will eventually cause him eye problems. It was almost comical trying to find a time that worked with the dentist's schedule.

    In between all of this and jumping through all of the hoops that we need to for Hallie's tethered cord surgery, we have been trying to fit in summer.. We went to Utah and absolutely loved every second of being with family. There is nothing like family! Hallie had to go off of her reflux meds our last week there so she was able to go swimming and run in the sprinklers and all sorts of fun things that she normally isn't able to do. It was so much fun to watch her enjoying all of it! This last weekend, we went camping to Jellystone with my in-laws. I cheated and didn't have her port accessed while we were there-- partly so she could swim, but mostly because I was worried about her keeping it clean while camping. She has turned into a fish the last few weeks. It is so fun to watch because water has never been her favorite thing. When you can't get in it for extended periods of time, it's hard to remember that it won't hurt you. We have 4 little girls on our street that will be going to kindergarten in Hallie's class. It is so fun to watch her get up in the morning and quickly straighten up her room and take her meds because she can't wait to go outside and play. Hallie's life up to this point has consisted of hospitals and very few friends. She has always been drawn to older kids-- usually teenage girls. It has been so fun to watch her developing friendships with so many different girls her same age. I looked out on my front porch yesterday to see 5 little girls sitting on the steps. This is a part of life that Hallie has little experience with and it is so much fun to watch her discover it all!

    The next few weeks we are taking it easy and working on the needs of the rest of the family. I actually am even going to see the dentist and optometrist-- novel concept in my world! By the time I deal with Hallie's needs and then the boys, I have so little time to take care of myself. My kids are loving having friends and going out to play every day. And I am enjoying a little bit of quiet time for myself. It's amazing the little things in life we take for granted! If nothing else, all of Hallie's medical problems have given us a better perspective on life! She came to me this morning and told me that she just realized how special she is. With all of the time she is spending with other kids her age, she is realizing how different she is, but at least she has a good attitude about it! Thank you for all of your prayers and support as we continue our fight for Miss Hallie. It means the world to us!

    • June 22, 2011

    Hallie has had a busy couple of weeks! Her CT scan and MRI went better than we could have hoped for. It was a good dry run for her before surgery. The anesthesiologists were perplexed by how she has been reacting to anesthesia (welcome to life with Hallie) so it took them awhile to figure out how to put her out, but I think we finally have it worked out. They gave her ketamine, which was originally used for horses. It's pretty scary to watch because it temporarily blinds her. We have used it as a pain med after surgery and I hated it! Luckily, they used it right before giving her the gases to make her fall asleep, so the blindness only lasted about a minute. You know your daughter has a reputation when you are the last scheduled case of the day and they bring you back to recovery as soon as she is done with the MRI, rather than let her come out of the anesthesia a little. She was in an isolation room, with a door on it, rather than the regular curtained room. They were discharging the last couple of patients as Hallie was brought back. Hallie's bed was completely padded, we had 2 nurses assigned to her and they had restraints on hand for when she woke up. So we waited, and waited, and waited. Hallie wasn't waking up. Derrick and I sat and talked to the nurses for awhile and finally they asked me if she still napped. I told them not always, but several times a week, when her body isn't doing very well. Hallie had slipped from being under the anesthesia into a nap! I finally woke her up because we had to go get her CT scan done.
